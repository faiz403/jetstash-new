Brand recovery

Three logo directions, one recommendation

The current circular "A" mark has no stated or visible relationship to "JetStash," a route, a waypoint, or stored travel intelligence. These three directions test alternatives. Only one is recommended for adoption.

Current mark (does not appear anywhere in this concept): a circle enclosing a peak/"A" shape with a dot beneath it. No customer could explain what it stands for without being told. It does not read as "J," "S," a route, or a waypoint.

Direction A — The Waypoint

Recommended — use this one

Two intersecting flight-path lines meeting at a single point: a literal route, and a negative-space "J" at header size. Ties directly to the proposition — every mark is a waypoint that has been verified. This is the direction used throughout the revised homepage concept; it is not left as an open choice.

The Waypoint

One colour, reads as a route plus a verified point, doubles as a "J" negative-space form. Thin, precise strokes read as instrumentation, not decoration.

Recommended for the full system.

Direction B — The Ledger Mark

A framed "J," referencing a stamped, checked document. Reads more corporate/formal than A; loses the direct "route" association.

Not recommended — secondary option only.

Direction C — The Arc

A single flight-path arc between two verified points. Reads closer to a generic "travel" glyph — the exact genre of mark this brief asked to avoid, one step removed from a clip-art aircraft path.

Rejected — too close to a generic travel icon.

Typography

Fraunces (display serif) restricted to the two true page-level headings on any given screen. Public Sans handles everything functional: body copy, evidence, prices, labels, forms, navigation, buttons, route names, verdict statements and legal copy.

Only the first line above is Fraunces. Every other line, including the verdict statement, evidence, price and caption, is Public Sans. This is the one concrete change from the first concept revision: verdict headlines and route-card titles were moved off the display face entirely.

Verification states do not use colour-coded backgrounds (no green "success" chip, no red "error" chip). Every state is ink or brass, distinguished by an icon and by wording, not by hue — this is what "verification states must not depend on colour alone" means in practice here.
